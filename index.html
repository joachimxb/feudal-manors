Feudal Manors — fees & the fighting men they buy

A sub-baronial enfeoffment module for ACKS II · esquire's fee to castellany, and the banners they muster · Hârn application in its own tab · the fee is quit of the garrison line by service — lance and levy — with the assize of the fee as its written terms

What this is. ACKS II's nobility stops at the 160-family baron; beneath sits the layer feudal armies were made of — the knight's fee. This module prices that layer from RAW economics alone: a fee is the smallest estate whose income pays its holder's wage (RR p.168), so the rungs fall out of the henchman wage table — ½ / 1 / 2 / 4 fees ↔ L2 / L3 / L4 / L5, each netting land + 3 gp per family, topping out where barony income begins. One structural change makes it feudal: the fee is quit of the garrison line by service — a lance and a trained levy, held to written terms (the assize of the fee, on every card), the host service bounded at one war-month a year in kind with extension at the lord's wages. Every ruling and its cost against strict RAW: the ⚖ doctrine table below and the Design tab. The sources and their dates: the History tab. Hârn, the first application, has its own tab.

Manors at a glance

Cost to lord = income he'd net keeping the land in demesne (garrison hired for cash → land+1 / land / land−1 per family). Lord's net = service value − cost: the surplus of enfeoffment is the garrison bill he no longer pays — knights profit him most on the frontier. The net buys a soldier, not liquidity — what enfeoffing gives up is itemized on each card. Each rung ≈ 2× the one below (wages double per level), so fees count in powers of two: 1 fee = L3, 2 = L4, 4 = L5.

Land value of the hex
3d3 per hex, rolled when the domain is secured (RR p.338) — average 6. A fee-holder nets land + 3 gp/family. The slider prices the floor at grant-time: on rich dirt a lord seats the same man on fewer families — and will, since every family he grants away costs him more demesne there. Standing manors don't shrink.
Ground under the fees — population density
RAW caps a 6-mile hex — ~31 sq mi, ~20,000 acres — at 185 / 375 / 780 families for outlands / borderlands / civilized (RR p.340, Limits of Growth). Density turns a fee's families into ground, and ground is what a lord actually parcels out of his hexes.
The assize of the fee — terms of enfeoffment
The quittance is conditional, and its conditions are written: mail per fee held (Assize of Arms 1181 — a knight owes as many hauberks as he holds fees; the sub-knightly man arms at the habergeon tier the assize set for its 10-mark freeman), the levy trained & kitted (the p.341 credit's own wording: "trained and equipped"), and a view of arms twice yearly (Statute of Winchester 1285) — held by the lord's officer under the inspecting-troops delegation (RR p.352–3; the Offices tab's ghost-soldier flag is this view, corrupted). A fee in default is quit for the lance only: it hires the levy's watch until it re-arms.
Fosterage — a squire rides with the lance
Knights foster their sons to other knights at ~14: the heir earns his levels before he inherits, and every lance gains a squire (~45 gp of medium-horse wage-value, RR p.169) at zero land cost — the boy's wage is his education. He keeps his knight horsed and stands the manor's watch, but takes no place in the line of battle. Reciprocal, so it nets out realm-wide. Formally the squire is his knight's henchman, and the fee is his license: a henchman may hold henchmen of his own only if landed with income ≥ 50% of his wage (RR p.168) — so the landed knight squires, and the landless household knight does not.

Reading the manors

Recruits is market logic — but it is consulted only at escheat: line extinct, fee forfeit, or new land enfeoffed. In the steady state the fee is heritable and reproduces its own holder — the heir squires out at ~14, inherits at L1–L2, and grows into his grade. Primogeniture's landless younger sons are, in turn, exactly the pool of market knights the recruitment logic prices. Wage-grade is the henchman rate the fee must cover to keep him once he holds it. Raises to is XP logic: a household (henchman) vassal subtracts his expected wage from domain income before the XP threshold applies (RR p.342 + p.423), so the fee grows its holder to one level below what it can retain — the last level is won in the field, never behind a plough. Knights are made by war; the fee only keeps them.

Two wage ladders meet here — know which is which. The mercenary table (60 gp regular / 100 gp veteran heavy horse) prices a soldier on the open market: kit, remounts, and no tenure. The henchman table (25 / 50 / 100 / 200 / 400) prices the stipend of a sworn man, whose loot-share and heritable tenure are the rest of his pay. They cross at 100 gp — a dubbed knight's market floor (the F1 veteran) equals an L3 stipend — which is exactly why one 12-family rung serves knights of L1–L3, and why the esquire's fee keeps an L2 veteran on 63 gp who would cost 100 as a hired lance: the tenure is the discount.

The fee is QUIT of the garrison line by service — the lance and the trained levy discharge it in kind, and the fee nets land+3. Nothing and no one is priced onto the line. The garrison line is a protection-value floor, not a payroll: RAW itself meets it with unpaid value — trained militia "even if the militia is not called up," scutage, a lord's troops (p.341–2) — though "garrison cost cannot be paid with expenditure on henchman… except actual troops." The form is a quittance, the period's own instrument: charters made estates quit of geld and of castle-guard in consideration of service kept, and quit-rent is the same deal run backward — coin paid to be quit of service. The fee exercises the scutage entry's equivalence standing: troops instead of gp, permanently. The Call-to-Arms duty (troops worth 1 gp/family, p.346) is what cash-vassalage would extract; the fee delivers a 100-gp lance instead.
Service in kind side-steps Tribute Inefficiency Cash tribute decays with vassal count (p.347): 9–16 vassals deliver 66%, 17–63 deliver 50%, 64–216 a third, 16,384+ just 1% (the Drakomir example). Only ≤8 vassals pay in full Service never passes through a collector's hands — the knight delivers himself. And the 100% band (≤8) coincides with the henchman cap (4+CHA): RAW's own economics wants the feudal shape — a handful of sworn tribute-vassals, and below them men who pay in person. A knight-heavy realm is poor in coin and rich in lances by design.
The sworn officer cures Tribute Inefficiency at a lawful 12.5% — the Offices tab's fisc oversight The inefficiency bands are unconditional: the loss goes to "crime, corruption, or waste," and nothing in RAW says appointing a steward recovers it. "The favors and duties even out; the rules put a standing army and feudal army on equal footing" (p.346–7). The household exception is the henchman bond: the inner circle rides without term. The office is the other way to set a man over land. RAW prices it already: a ruler may appoint magistrates (RR p.342) — captain of the guard, chaplain, munerator, steward — each overseeing one line of the domain's expenses and keeping 12.5% of the line he oversees as salary. The salary is carved out of the line itself (the ruler pays nothing extra and the troops are not reduced), it counts as the magistrate's own domain income for campaign XP, and RAW wants him sworn: magistrates are "usually fellow adventurers, followers, or henchmen," and "non-henchman magistrates might be susceptible to corruption or treachery." The sheriff and the constable of a royal castle are this machinery set over a district rather than the ruler's whole domain, overseeing two lines: the crown's tribute gathered through the castle, and the garrison it pays for. Command rides with the second line — the captain-of-the-guard delegations are inspecting troops, overseeing training, training troops, and waging war (RR p.352–3).

This is doctrine, declared: RAW's four magistrates oversee expense lines only, and none collects revenue — we add the fisc as a fifth oversight for district officers. RAW rewards the extension twice. Cash tribute decays with vassal count (Tribute Inefficiency, RR p.347): a third to half of every payment is lost to "crime, corruption, or waste" — and a sworn collector on a lawful 12.5% is exactly the cure the period applied. And the office is the anti-fee: paid in coin not land, revocable not heritable, loyal at 0 + the crown's CHA where the enfeoffed mass sits at −2 — which is why crowns ran castles by commission while seating knights on fees. The opposite pole — the officer who keeps everything above a fixed render, as the early English sheriffs farmed their shires — is the Judges Journal's landowner/governor split (JJ p.405–6); this counter prices the reformed, salaried custodian instead. And where the office is not held by a sworn man, RAW's warning gets numbers: the appointee skims a share of the spread — the gap between his lawful fee and the district's inefficiency band — sliding the crown's take from 87.5% back down toward the band itself. The two RAW tables are the endpoints; loyalty is the dial between them.

The dial at a glance — this district, the whole loyalty ladder

A vocabulary note first: the dial runs on the officer's loyalty — the man's stat (base −2 unsworn, −4 beyond the range of trade, RR p.347; "hostile" is the Hostility result of the Henchman Loyalty table) — not morale, which in ACKS II belongs to the district's population (base morale score, RR p.346–8). Read the endpoints: the sworn man costs the lawful 12.5% (RR p.342); the hostile man delivers the crown exactly the inefficiency band (RR p.347) — RAW resumes when governance fails. Loyalty prices everything between. And because salary and skim alike are the officer's income for XP (p.342/423), corruption levels the man: the unsworn officer grows himself out of the crown's own revenue stream — a self-funded villain, which is how a Sheriff of Nottingham happens. The gap between any row and the sworn man's row is what the bond is worth: closing it is what fosterage and household office-holding are for. The ladder is generated by one rule — an unsworn officer's skim share is (2 − loyalty) ÷ 8, clamped 0–1: at +2 good lordship collects like the sworn man, and by −6 the band has returned without waiting for a Hostility roll. And mind the render rate: the spread scales with the take, so the unsworn officer who exacts 2 gp/family where custom says 1 doubles fee and skim alike — raising the taxes can raise the man a level. Nottingham squeezed the shire for a reason.

What this module does

ACKS II's Titles of Nobility stop at the 160-family baron; everything beneath is a single undifferentiated "domain." But the layer beneath the baron is where feudal armies actually came from — the knight's fee. This module prices that layer with RAW economics and nothing else: the henchman wage table fixes what a man of each level must be paid; a fee is the smallest estate whose income pays him (RR p.168 — a henchman-vassal "does not need to be paid a wage if the domain income is equal to or greater than his wage"); the mercenary tables set the recruitment floor. From those inputs alone fall the rung sizes, the powers-of-two "counting in fees" idiom, the lord's profit from enfeoffment, and the XP plateaus. Where the model must choose between ACKS's classical default — free-floating rulers paying cash tribute — and the feudal pattern, it chooses feudal and says so: every such ruling is quoted against RAW in the ⚖ doctrine table on the Manors tab. History enters as validation only — if the reconstruction is honest, its ratios should match the medieval record without ever being tuned to it.

Lineage

This is the third generation of an idea. The Wandering Gamist's "ACKS Estates – Nobility" (2017) first worked the knight's fee in ACKS terms: a ~10-family estate as the support unit of a lance, with the decisive observation that feudal service is seasonal — which is why land-for-service beats year-round wages. Helgeran's Knightly Realms systematized it into a whole pyramid: uniform 10-family knightholds under 2nd-level knights netting land+1 (the garrison still paid in cash, centralized as the baron's 852-gp foot), banners of fifteen fees run as micro-senates, and service in place of tribute below the viscount. This module refines toward the historical record: a graduated scale of manors (esquire to castellan) sized by wage-parity instead of uniform knightholds; the garrison taken in kind (land+3 — the knight's own lance and trained yeomen in place of the coin line); heritable fees with fosterage demographics closing the XP loop; fidelity through the household rather than the senate; and the validation panel (History tab). This version states the doctrine as a quittance — the fee is quit of the garrison line so long as its service is kept, the period's own instrument (estates quit of geld or castle-guard for service; quit-rent is the same deal run backward) — and writes the conditions down as the assize of the fee: mail per fee held, levy trained and kitted, view of arms twice yearly, with default priced on every card.

The engine — levels first, history second

Be clear about the causality: these manors are generated by ACKS character levels, not by history. The engine is the henchman wage table (RR p.168), which doubles per level — 25 / 50 / 100 / 200 / 400 gp — and one farming fact: a fee-holder who garrisons his own land in kind nets land + 3 gp per family (9 at average land). Divide wage by net and the rungs fall out: ~7, 12, 23, 45 families. (That 23 misses 2 × 12 by one on purpose: grants come in whole families, so each rung rounds its own remainder up — two bachelor's fees carry two rounding pads where one double fee carries one. The same arithmetic prices two esquire's fees at 14 families against the bachelor's 12: consolidation wastes less remainder, one more reason lords preferred it — and real fee assessments never doubled cleanly either.) Richer land shrinks these floors, as it should — fees were income assessments, and the hide itself was physically smaller in fertile shires; but a standing manor never resizes, it re-grades: the same estate assesses at more fees and keeps a better knight (see the lever's standing-manor line). Because the wage doubles per level, each rung is twice the one below — which is why the medieval idiom of "holding N fees" maps perfectly onto levels: ½ fee = L2-grade, 1 fee = L3, 2 fees = L4, 4 fees = L5. The manors stop at L5 because the next doubling (800 gp) is a barony's income (160 families × 7 = 1,120 gp, plateauing at L6 against the 1,200 XP threshold): a level-6 man is a lord, not a knight. History enters as validation — if the reconstruction is honest, its ratios should land inside the medieval record's own ranges. The cross-checks (History tab) show where they hit exactly and where they sit at the edge of the band. A fee is, throughout, a job posting with a fixed salary: its income must match what the man's sword earns on the open market, or he rides elsewhere.

FAQ — the questions this module gets asked
QuestionAnswer
Can a ruler really count his own person against garrison cost? Said plainly: this is the module's structural break with RAW — one line, bounded, priced. Strict RAW puts a garrison line on every domain and treats it as a floor to be paid (its own aside pictures a ruler "spending only the minimum 2gp," p.342). This module rules the fee quit of that line so long as its service is kept: the lance and the trained levy are the consideration, on the model of charters that made estates quit of geld or castle-guard for service kept, and on RAW's own printed equivalence — "a vassal may send troops instead of gp" (p.346) — exercised standing. The size of the break: at civilized, full militia credit zeroes the line by pure RAW; the entire concession is the frontier increment, ~1–2 gp per family. What the quittance form buys is not a smaller break but a cleaner one — the discharge is contractual, so the knight is never priced onto the line and the "what is he worth" argument never starts; and it has teeth: a fee in default of the assize is quit for the lance only and hires its missing watch until it re-arms. Asked who actually defends the manor: one knight, his squire, and his armed yeomen (the full ruling: ⚖ doctrine table, Manors tab).
Isn't that minting XP at eight times the magistrate's rate? RAW already mints campaign XP from the garrison line for a military man: the captain of the guard's salary is carved from garrison expenditure, "does not decrease the funds available to pay his troops," and "grants campaign XP" (p.342) — precedent for the kind of move, not the amount. Against the RAW-legal baseline — militia credit maxed — the quittance books 0 extra XP-bearing income at civilized, 12 gp at borderlands, 24 at outlands, beside the captain's lawful 4.5–6 gp on those same lines: between 0× and 4×, and less than RAW at civilized. Nor are the two equally secured: the captain's eighth is free money — troops unreduced, nothing extra delivered — while the fee's income books only while the consideration is delivered: a veteran lance and a trained levy actually standing there, on terms the view of arms audits twice a year. Per gp of XP, one buys the realm a garrison, the other an accountant. And a 12.5% share presupposes a domain with a staff; at fee scale the org chart collapses — captain, garrison, and ruler are one man with one purse. You can't pay a man an eighth of himself.
Is XP double-counted — does the knights' campaign XP subtract from their lord's? No gold piece appears on two XP ledgers. The knight XPs on his own domain income minus expected wage (p.342); the lord books nothing from the fee — no tribute, no coin; and the service render, like RAW's own scutage, "does not count as domain income for XP purposes" (p.346). A service realm concentrates less XP up top than a tribute realm — the coin and its XP stay on the land, in the knights. That distribution is the Arthurian texture, chosen on purpose.
Doesn't the knight piggyback on his lord's Personal Authority? No rule shares PA — it is computed per ruler, per domain. And the fees sit on the PA-competence diagonal by construction: the PA table's income columns are the GP-threshold bands (verified cell-by-cell against p.348), so the free holder at his plateau reads exactly PA 0 over his own manor — the book's own Designer's Note says rulers advance to PA 0 and stop. One honest nuance: the household-raised man, one level below his fee's grade, reads −1 until the field finishes him — which is the module's texture, not a leak: the last level is won at war (cross-checks, History tab). Nothing is borrowed, because nothing is missing.
Doesn't the in-kind garrison inflate knightly income? Run the challenge's own formula (land + 4 + 2 − 3 − garrison + militia) at its RAW limit — full militia credit, 2 per 10 families at 12 gp, good even uncalled — and it yields land+3 exactly at civilized class. The entire dispute is the frontier increment, 1–2 gp per family; concede it whole and the bachelor's fee moves from 12 families to 13 at borderlands. What the gold per family buys is a sworn knight in the manor house above the armed peasantry — and p.432 (rebels are drawn from the militia when a domain turns) is exactly why the assize pairs the levy with a resident lord: the counterweight is a term of the same contract.
The lineage knight is no henchman — why does the wage table bind him? Because the wage table is the market price of a level-L man, and the fee must pay it at every point of contact. At a fee's birth from the household, p.168 applies verbatim: the lord enfeoffs a knight he was already paying, and land replaces wage. At a birth from the market — RAW lets a lord enfeoff non-henchmen outright: "mercenary commanders, adventurers" (p.347) — the fee's income is the recruitment offer, and it must meet the man's open-market wage or he rides on. And at every inheritance the market binds the heirs: a manor yielding 96 gp doesn't hold a 100-gp man across generations — his brothers take service for wages, and standing manors re-grade rather than resize. Netting, offering, retaining: one price, three enforcement points.
Who pays when the levy is called up? Someone does: the provider — RAW's general law (Call to Arms: the vassal musters troops at his own wage cost; Troops favor: the lord pays for what he sends down, p.346). The rung cards price it: afield, the knight pays his yeomen's wages and their plough-teams stand idle. The idle plough is booked at the family's full revenue rate (land+6), because RAW's two ledger columns come apart here: p.432 reduces domain revenues by one family per man levied ("produce revenue as if it has [fewer] families"), while p.342's Costs per Family column — liturgies, tithe, maintenance — keeps charging: the family still lives there; only its production marches. Net loss per man is therefore the whole revenue, not the fee's net take. Together roughly two-fifths of the fee's income per war-month, plus −2 morale — free to keep, dear to call, which is why historical levies were short-term, defensive instruments. And the bill is bounded: the fee owes the term — one war-month a year, the forty days — at its own cost; past it the muster rides on the lord's wages (the pay ladder, 1s/2s/4s ↔ 60/100/200), as the rolls show fee-holders drawing. The knight himself is pre-paid for the term: the fee sustains him year-round — at home always, in the host for the forty days, and beyond them at the lord's coin.

Played strictly by RAW, a sub-baronial vassal pays his lord in coin, and the arithmetic leaves a low-level man on a big manor — out of the box, ACKS builds a classical realm. This module's structural change is one: the fee renders service instead of coin and is quit of the garrison line for it, at a cost of about one gold piece per family against strict accounting. Around it stand a set of explicit rulings — no per-fee tribute, service booked at full wage, the assize, the squire, heroic-unit pricing, the fisc oversight and its corruption dial — each quoted against RAW in the ⚖ doctrine table (Manors tab). Count them all and the honest tally is nine rulings, not one: the difference is that the rest are extensions where RAW prices nothing, not overrides of what it does.

Making sense for the lord — the other half of the schema

A rung only exists if the lord profits by granting it — otherwise no fee is ever enfeoffed and the manors are a fantasy. The ledger closes from his side too, and it is two-colored: demesne families garrisoned in coin, enfeoffed families in men — demesne × the garrison rate in cash, plus one lance per fee, and no garrison line under the fees at all. That is the servitium debitum's own arithmetic: a quota of knights, never a commuted garrison budget. His cost is the demesne income foregone (land+1 / land / land−1 per family — in his hands the garrison is paid in cash); the service he receives is the holder's market rate (plus the fosterage squire). At wage-parity fees his net is almost exactly families × the garrison rate (2/3/4): the entire surplus of enfeoffment is the garrison bill he no longer pays. That is why the deal is stable from both sides — the man can get no better wage elsewhere, and the lord turns a profit at every rung, deepest on the frontier where garrisons cost most. It is also why frontier lords historically seeded knights along the marches.

Three more load-bearing integration points. Tribute: assessed once, in cash, on the lord's whole realm (18 × families0.6, RR p.343–4); knights owe service, not coin, so there is no per-fee tribute line — the same lance garrisons the fee and answers the lord's call. This is doctrine (see the ⚖ rulings table, Manors tab), but RAW rewards it twice over: the tribute formula was never calibrated below ~100 families (on a 12-family fee it would confiscate 74% of income), and cash tribute decays with vassal count — Tribute Inefficiency (RR p.347) hands the collectors a third of every payment at 9–16 vassals, half at 17–63, and 99% in the Drakomir example's 21,600 — while service in kind never passes through a collector's hands. Only ≤8 vassals pay in full, and that band coincides with the henchman cap: RAW's own economics wants the feudal shape — a handful of sworn tribute-vassals, and below them, men who pay in person. Density: England ran ~1 knight's fee per ~170 households (dubbed knights were rarer still) because crown, church, and demesne held half to two-thirds of the land un-enfeoffed, and many fees were held by scutage-paying esquires, widows, and wards — the demesne lever in the lord calculator is exactly that dial. The levy: ACKS's militia rules (2 per 10 families, qualifying numbers, kit and skills passed to heirs — RR p.432) are the Assize of Arms of 1181 in game form — down to the statute's arithmetic: the Assize owed a hauberk per fee held, and the rungs' heavy-foot yeomen at land 6 count 0/1/2/4 on the ½/1/2/4-fee rungs (see the cross-checks). The module writes this into the assize of the fee on every card — the levy as a term of enfeoffment, audited by the twice-yearly view of arms (Winchester 1285) that the Offices tab's inspection delegation already staffs — and prices default: a fee that lets its levy lapse is quit for the lance only, hiring its watch until it re-arms. And the qualifying numbers enforce the deepest structural rule: the first levied heavy horseman needs a 60-family pool, so the levy cannot make a knight — knights are made by land, footmen by levy.

Scutage — the quittance run backward

The fee's standing deal is a coin-obligation discharged by service; scutage is the same equivalence exercised the other way, for the term — the year's war-month discharged in coin (both directions are printed in the one entry, RR p.346). In the two-colored ledger the fee changes color for one war: its render arrives as coin, and the lord hires a replacement into the host. The exchange always trades down — the lawful floor is the Call-to-Arms quota (1 gp/family, p.346), the market replacement is a 60-gp heavy horseman, and what the host lost was a 100–400 gp wage-grade veteran — which is why lords prefer the men, and why the rates ratcheted for two centuries. Two things scutage never buys out: the assize (readiness is a condition of the tenure, not of a campaign — the view of arms runs regardless), and the XP ledger ("scutage does not count as domain income for XP purposes," p.346 — the coin as XP-inert as the service it replaces).

Who pays it is the elegant part: the scutage-payer's lance stays home, so the natural clientele is history's own — wardships, widows, dotards, the infirm: fees that cannot currently deliver a man. That is the quittance's lawful patch for the minority — the wardship pays coin until the heir can ride, the tenure never lapses, and the manor is meanwhile the best-defended fee in the realm, its lance never leaving the yard. The household inner circle rides, for their bond is the service; scutage is the instrument of the lineage mass and the incapable. And at realm scale it is the era table's dial: one commuted campaign is a footnote, but a realm where scutage becomes the norm has slid from the distraint century into the indenture century (the era table, History tab) — service melting into coin, on schedule. The module does not forbid the slide; it prices it and names the destination. Scutage works here as what it was: the escape valve whose overuse is the end of the system.

What the fee buys that coin cannot

An army in being, not an army in waiting. In RAW an army is not decided into existence — it is recruited: each realm holds a finite crop of every mercenary type, surfaced only by paid searching and replenished on the scale of seasons (RR p.428). The crops are small — a barony's crop contains no heavy horseman at all, and even a kingdom's 680 heavy horse take three seasons to muster — and the Judges Journal's recruitment vagaries can double the price or empty the market outright. Coin stores value, not readiness. The fee is the period's own solution: it plants a warrior tradition that maintains itself — the knight sits armed, mounted, and obliged on his own ground; his heir squires out and comes home trained; his yeomen pass kit and skills to their sons (RR p.432). A lord of enfeoffed fees rides the week the beacon lights, at the strength the manors price; his cash-rich neighbor is still paying finding fees to learn what troops exist.

And the realm it builds feels heroic — on purpose. Run a realm's military surplus through fees instead of wage-troops and its host fills with leveled men: banners of L1–L5 knights fight as heroic units among the companies of foot (RR p.466), worth several times their weight in hired horse. In peacetime those same men are out and about — errantry, tournament, escort, feud — because a fee obliges a man, not a garrison post. This is true to the period, and it is a dial on tone: an ACKS campaign run on knightly manors drifts toward Arthurian epic — named riders on every field, and adventure quartered in every manor.

Fidelity — whose man is he?

RAW grades vassals three ways, and the manors use all three. The henchman vassal (base loyalty 0 + the lord's CHA) is the household officer — but a lord holds at most 4 + CHA henchmen, so the inner circle is small: the banneret, the castellan, the marshal. At 9th level with a stronghold a magnate attracts companions (RR p.334–6): L1–3 men who "function as henchmen for all purposes" including vassalage, occupy no henchman slot, and start at +2 loyalty — the crown's and the earls' instrument for seating household knights on fees. Everyone else — every inherited fee — is a non-henchman vassal (RR p.347): base loyalty −2, and −4 if his manor lies beyond the range of trade of the lord's largest town (JJ p.203 — the trade maps double as loyalty maps). No free duty: one duty per favor, so service must be continuously bought with good lordship — gifts, offices, monopolies, marriages. The term divides the tiers, too: the household rides without limit — that bond is the oath, not the fee — while the enfeoffed mass owes the year's war-month, then wages. These "penalties" are the medieval texture, not a defect: enfeoffed fidelity was contractual, not devotional — homage, distraint, and forfeiture all exist because enfeoffed men defaulted. And a pillaged fee is an unpaid knight (going without wages is a calamity → loyalty roll), so frontier defeats cascade into treason exactly as they should.

Fosterage is the loyalty technology. An heir inherits his father's fee, not his father's bond — by default he is a −2 stranger with a claim. Squiring him at the lord's court is what re-bonds each generation: he comes home a made man of the household, eligible to be taken as henchman or companion. The fosterage lever is fidelity manufacture as much as XP delivery. The henchman cap also forces subinfeudation (RR p.343, the Quintus example): a baron holds four or five castellans and double-fee knights close, and they hold the bachelors — the pyramid is the only legal shape. Knightly Realms solved the same problem the other way: banners of fifteen fees organized as micro-senates (RR p.355), knights voting, base loyalty 0 — equally RAW. Choose by flavor: the household, or the hundred-court.

Levels drive everything — and the XP loop closes

The rung sizes are retention prices: the fee that matches a level's wage. ACKS's XP rules then close the career loop — differently per fidelity tier. A henchman or companion vassal subtracts his expected wage from domain income before the XP threshold applies (RR p.342 + p.423; the wage is "expected" — imputed whether or not coin changes hands), so his fee raises him to one level below what it retains — the 12-family fee pays an L3 but only grows an L2, and the last level must be won in the field. A lineage (non-henchman) vassal subtracts nothing and ploughs his way to the fee's full grade — the country knight's career. (One degeneracy at the ladder's foot, marked on the card: on the esquire's fee the two accountings coincide at L2, because L1's wage and L1's threshold are both 25 gp — the esquire sits below the level where household accounting bites.) Fosterage is how the field reaches the heir before he inherits either way: squired out at ~14, he comes home L1–L2 and grows into his grade. Domain XP alone is glacial (the esquire's fee needs ~13 quiet years to make its man a L2 veteran) — which is the correct result: knights are made by war; the fee only keeps them.

How history is used here

History enters this module as validation, never as input: the rung sizes were derived from the ACKS wage table alone, then checked against the medieval record. This tab holds the record side — the anchors, the cross-checks, the century readings, the honest caveats — and grades every claim by how hard its source is. Three grades recur: table-verified (checked cell-by-cell against ACKS II's printed tables, 2nd printing plus Major Errata r4), statute / record (a dated instrument of the period, with manuscript variance disclosed where it exists), and scholarship (standard secondary figures, flagged where not independently re-verified). The full grading sits in Sources & confidence at the bottom — no two claims here are asserted at the same strength unless they deserve it.

A campaign can therefore set its century. Domesday is this module at poor land and small household grants; the high ladder is the thirteenth century; a realm that commutes to scutage and indenture is the fifteenth. The dials even keep the eras honest in combination: a 40-family estate at land 6 seats an L4 county knight; at land 3 it can still pay an L4 but no longer grows one; and seating an L2 on it builds a thirteenth-century estate under a Domesday-grade man — an estate two centuries older than its holder, a pairing the record never produced. The one thing the ladder refuses to model is a century in which fee sizes and knightly grades drift apart: the record says they never did.

Hârn — the first application

Kaldor is modeled on 12th–13th-century England, so most of the fit is inheritance — the manors were derived from ACKS RAW, and Hârn's canon institutions land on their rungs without adjustment. Worked example — Touvant Manor, canon's own pick

Kingdom of Kaldor demonstrates its levy on a named manor (Kaldor 21): "The knight of Touvant Manor (Heru), has 2220 acres and thus owes 1mh (himself), 1lh (a squire), plus five yeomen (2mf and 3lf) and scutage for 120 acres." The fief table (Kaldor 30) fills in the rest: Endrunel Hundred, held in fee simple by clan Plaganel under Heru Keep and the Earl of Osel — 2,220 gross acres · land quality 1.21 · 37 households. Because canon chose this manor itself, running it through the module is a like-for-like test nobody picked for convenience.

Kaldor at kingdom scale

Kaldor's ~17,000 families yield a feudal host of armored men in the meaningful hundreds — consistent with how the Kingdom of Kaldor module portrays its musters.
